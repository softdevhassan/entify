The Teacher’s Challenge

Our journey began when Sir Abdur-Rehman, who teaches us Artificial Intelligence, announced a major project focused on Natural Language Processing. He didn’t just give us a list of tasks. Instead, he told us to explore the field, find a problem worth solving, and then present a formal proposal as a group.

After we shared our plan to build a comparative system for Name Entity Recognition, he sat with us and reviewed the technical scope. His feedback was incredibly encouraging, and with a simple "this is a good project," he officially approved our 8-week developmental cycle.

Deciding on a Topic

It took us a lot of brainstorming to find the right focus. In the beginning, we actually explored several different paths, including building a fake news detector, a social media sentiment tool, and even image or audio-voice detectors. However, each of those had major drawbacks for an 8-week production sprint.

We realized that image and audio detection were moving too far away from pure Natural Language Processing. They would have required a whole set of extra libraries for computer vision and audio processing that weren't the core focus of our study. For fake news, we found that models are usually trained on fixed datasets, but the world changes so fast that a truly useful tool would need real-time data which is very hard to maintain.

Even social media analysis was tricky because the necessary APIs from platforms like Facebook are often quite costly. We wanted a project that wasn't just a prototype but could actually be "production-ready" by the time we finished. Named Entity Recognition allowed exactly that. It has a stable, fixed dataset in CoNLL2003, allowing us to build a robust, professional system that functions perfectly on a live server.

Growing as a Team

The team dynamics were quite interesting during the first two weeks. While Hassan Ali already had experience as a developer, Mudassir and Saad Ilyas had to start from the ground up.

They spent those early days diving deep into Python OOP, mastering how to manage code with Git, and learning the fundamentals of Tkinter for desktop design. It was intensive, but it ensured everyone was ready when the real coding started.

The Last-Minute Pivot

Believe it or not, we actually finished a complete desktop version of this tool using Tkinter by the sixth week. It worked well and had a nice interface.

But since we were already studying Web Technologies at the time, we felt that a web platform would be much more impactful. We spent the final two weeks redesigning everything in Flask and deploying it on an AWS-EC2 server at ner.orbin.dev to make it accessible to everyone.

A Note on BIO Tagging

To identify phrases that contain multiple words, like "Pakistan Cricket Board," our model uses what's called the BIO scheme. It stands for Beginning, Inside, and Outside.

B (Beginning)

Marks the very first word of a name or entity.

I (Inside)

Marks every word that follows within that same entity.

O (Outside)

Marks common words that aren't entities at all.

Interesting Findings

One of the coolest things we noticed was how the models handle ambiguity. For example, our CRF model sometimes called "Google" a location because it often followed the word "at" in the news articles it learned from. This helped us understand why neural networks like spaCy are often more accurate in complex situations.
